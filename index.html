Reducing Simulator Sickness in Virtual Reality with Haptic Feedback

Learn more

Tackling an ever present problem in Virtual Reality.

Introduction

VR has seen a surge in popularity in recent years, yet the same simulator sickness that has plagued VR since its inception, remains a problem today. Simulator sickness is a form of motion sickness experienced in simulators and VR. Symptoms of simulator sickness, similar to that of motion sickness but less severe, include: nausea, headache, blurred vision, and eye-strain A major contributing factor behind simulator sickness is vection - vection is best described as a feeling of self motion. We aim to reduce vection by the use of haptic feedback via the design and implementation of two systems, a stepper and a tether, which will be compared to traditional controller-based movement.

The stepper is a walking-in-place form of locomotion, and builds upon existing research. The second is a tether device which is a combination of walking-in-place and gesture-based control and is, to the best of our knowledge, an unexplored method. Both these systems aim to reduce simulator sickness by providing additional sensory information in the form of haptic feedback.

The Experiment

During the experiment the user walks through a custom-made environment. The environment was developed using the Unity game engine. The environment is intended to increase the likelihood of one experiencing simulator sickness, and needs to be monodirectional (so that it is compatible with our movement methods). Thus we place the user on a straight path in a forest. The forest helps induce simulator sickness as the density of trees on the sides of the path increase peripheral vision activity. This in turn helps induce vection. We use low-poly textures to ensure that the frame rate is constantly higher than 90fps, as we want to limit the amount of application design factors that affect simulator sickness.

Our main measure for simulator sickness is the Simulator Sickness Questionnaire (SSQ) developed by Kennedy et al. [13]. Each subject took a baseline SSQ and then a further 6 - one before and after each movement method. A subject took 2 SSQs instead of 1 before the controller as they would play through an acclimatisation game after their baseline SSQ and thus we had to ensure that the acclimatisation game did not affect our controllers SSQ scores.

We were also interested in the correlation between our SSQ scores and the relevant physiological indicators that were found to correlate with simualtor sickness. The physiological measures we assessed are heart rate, respiratory rate, and skin conductance level (SCL). These measures were recorded with a Biopac measurement device.

We performed a single factor repeated measures experiment with the factor being the type of movement method. Subjects used each method on a different day and the order of the methods were fixed: a subject always used the controller, then the tether, and lastly the stepper.

Results

Conclusions

This paper found that the stepper did not reduce simulator sickness in VR but rather increased it. This is likely due to the extra effort required to use the stepper and due to the instability subject experienced. This paper also found that the tether does not provide any additional benefit nor disadvantage when compared to the controller.

Ideas for potential future work involve the use of variable tensile strength for the tether, the use of two tethers for two-dimensional movement. We do not recommend the use of a stepper for future experiments as the physical effort required by the user ultimately detracts from any possible benefit.
